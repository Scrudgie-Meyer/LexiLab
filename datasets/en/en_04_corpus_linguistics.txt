Corpus linguistics is a methodology in linguistics that involves the large scale analysis of authentic language use based on collections of texts known as corpora. A corpus is a principled collection of texts assembled for the purpose of linguistic investigation. Modern corpora typically contain millions or even billions of words, enabling researchers to identify patterns that would be invisible in smaller samples.

The field of corpus linguistics emerged in the nineteen sixties, coinciding with the development of computational tools capable of processing large amounts of text. Early corpora such as the Brown Corpus, which contained one million words of American English, were revolutionary for their time. Contemporary reference corpora like the British National Corpus and the Corpus of Contemporary American English contain hundreds of millions of words.

Frequency analysis is the foundation of corpus-based linguistic research. By counting how often words and grammatical structures occur in a corpus, linguists can establish what is typical or unusual in a language. Frequency information is essential for second language teaching, lexicography, and the development of natural language processing systems.

Collocations, the habitual co-occurrence of words, are one of the most important areas of corpus linguistic research. Native speakers intuitively know that we say heavy rain rather than strong rain, and make a decision rather than do a decision, even though both alternatives might seem logical. Corpus analysis reveals these preferences quantitatively and helps explain why certain word combinations feel natural while others sound foreign.

Key word analysis compares the frequency of words in a specialized corpus against a reference corpus to identify the vocabulary that is particularly characteristic of the specialized domain. Words that occur significantly more often in the specialized corpus than in the reference corpus are identified as keywords. This method has been applied to study the distinctive vocabulary of academic writing, legal language, medical texts, and many other registers.

Discourse analysis examines how language is organized at the level of connected text and conversation, beyond the sentence boundary. Corpus-based discourse analysis investigates patterns of coherence, cohesion, and topic structure across large collections of texts. Studies have revealed systematic patterns in how academic papers are organized, how arguments are constructed in different cultures, and how spoken conversation differs from written text.

Grammatical studies using corpora have challenged many traditional assumptions about language structure. Constructions that were described in grammar books as rare or ungrammatical turn out to be common in authentic usage. Conversely, patterns that were assumed to be general turn out to be restricted to particular registers or styles. Corpus evidence has forced a more nuanced understanding of grammatical variation.

Sociolinguistic variation, the systematic differences in language use associated with social factors such as age, gender, region, and social class, has been extensively studied using corpus methods. Large corpora allow researchers to distinguish genuine linguistic change from random variation and to track the progress of language change over time. Diachronic corpora containing texts from different historical periods enable the study of language change directly.

Learner corpus research investigates the language produced by second language learners and compares it to native speaker production. Learner corpora reveal systematic patterns of errors, overuse of certain constructions, and avoidance of others. These findings inform the development of pedagogical materials and language assessment instruments for second language teaching.

The development of web corpora, assembled by automatically crawling the internet, has dramatically expanded the quantity of language data available for linguistic research. Web corpora provide access to text in hundreds of languages and dialects, including many languages for which no traditional corpora existed. Despite their size advantage, web corpora present challenges related to text quality, duplication, and representativeness that continue to be addressed by researchers.
