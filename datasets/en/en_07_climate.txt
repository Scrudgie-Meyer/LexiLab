Climate change represents one of the most significant challenges facing humanity in the twenty-first century. The scientific consensus, based on decades of research and observation, is that the Earth's climate system is warming primarily due to human activities, especially the burning of fossil fuels. Rising temperatures are already causing measurable changes in precipitation patterns, sea levels, extreme weather events, and ecosystems around the world.

The greenhouse effect is the fundamental mechanism driving climate change. Greenhouse gases such as carbon dioxide, methane, and nitrous oxide trap heat in the atmosphere that would otherwise escape into space. Before industrialization, the natural greenhouse effect maintained a stable climate suitable for life. Human emissions have enhanced this effect, causing the atmosphere to retain more energy and global average temperatures to rise.

Global surface temperatures have increased by approximately one point one degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels. The rate of warming has accelerated in recent decades. The last decade was the hottest on record globally, and individual years have repeatedly broken temperature records. Ocean temperatures are also rising, and the oceans have absorbed more than ninety percent of the excess heat trapped by greenhouse gases.

Sea level rise poses serious risks to coastal communities worldwide. Thermal expansion of warming ocean water and the melting of ice sheets in Greenland and Antarctica are the primary contributors. Global mean sea level has risen by about twenty centimeters since eighteen eighty. Without significant emissions reductions, sea level could rise by a meter or more by the end of this century, threatening hundreds of millions of people living in low-lying coastal areas.

Extreme weather events are becoming more frequent and intense in many regions. Heat waves are more severe and prolonged. Heavy precipitation events are more intense due to the increased moisture-holding capacity of warmer air. Drought conditions are worsening in many regions. Hurricanes and typhoons are intensifying more rapidly. These changes impose substantial economic costs and endanger human lives.

Biodiversity loss is accelerating as climate change disrupts ecosystems. Species are shifting their ranges toward the poles and to higher elevations in response to warming. Coral reefs, which support extraordinary biodiversity, are bleaching and dying as ocean temperatures rise. Phenological mismatches, where the timing of biological events like flowering and pollination no longer coincides, threaten the stability of ecological relationships that have evolved over millions of years.

Mitigation of climate change requires rapid and deep reductions in greenhouse gas emissions. The transition to renewable energy sources such as solar and wind power is underway in many countries. Electric vehicles are replacing internal combustion engines. Energy efficiency improvements in buildings, industry, and transportation can significantly reduce emissions. Carbon capture and storage technologies may play a role in removing carbon dioxide from the atmosphere.

Climate adaptation involves adjusting to the changes that are already underway or inevitable. Infrastructure must be designed to withstand more extreme weather. Agricultural practices must adapt to shifting precipitation patterns and longer growing seasons in some regions. Coastal cities must plan for sea level rise. Public health systems must prepare for new disease patterns and heat related illness.

International cooperation is essential for effective climate action. The Paris Agreement, adopted in two thousand and fifteen, established a global framework for reducing emissions. Countries committed to limit warming to well below two degrees Celsius compared to pre-industrial levels. However, current national commitments are insufficient to meet this goal, and far more ambitious action is needed from all major emitters.

Young people around the world are demanding urgent action on climate change. The climate justice movement emphasizes that those who have contributed least to climate change often suffer its worst consequences. Indigenous communities, developing nations, and future generations bear disproportionate risks from a changing climate caused primarily by wealthy industrialized countries. Addressing this injustice alongside emissions reductions is increasingly recognized as essential to the legitimacy of climate policy.
