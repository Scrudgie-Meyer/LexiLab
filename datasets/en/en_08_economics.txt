Economics is the social science that studies how individuals, firms, governments, and societies allocate scarce resources. The discipline is divided into microeconomics, which examines the behavior of individual agents and markets, and macroeconomics, which studies economy-wide phenomena such as inflation, unemployment, and economic growth. Both branches use mathematical models and empirical methods to understand economic behavior and inform policy decisions.

Market economies coordinate economic activity through the price system. When the price of a good rises, producers are incentivized to supply more while consumers demand less, naturally balancing supply and demand. This decentralized coordination mechanism, described by Adam Smith as the invisible hand, allows complex economies to function without central planning. However, markets sometimes fail to produce efficient outcomes, providing a rationale for government intervention.

Gross domestic product is the standard measure of economic output, representing the total market value of all goods and services produced within a country in a given period. Economic growth, measured as the percentage change in gross domestic product, is closely watched by policymakers and investors. Per capita gross domestic product provides a rough measure of living standards, though it does not capture distribution of income or non-market activities.

Monetary policy refers to the actions taken by central banks to manage the money supply and interest rates in order to achieve macroeconomic objectives. When inflation is high, central banks raise interest rates to slow borrowing and spending. When unemployment is high, central banks lower interest rates to stimulate economic activity. The Federal Reserve in the United States, the European Central Bank, and other central banks use a variety of tools to implement monetary policy.

Fiscal policy refers to government decisions about spending and taxation. Government spending on infrastructure, education, and healthcare can boost economic growth and productivity. Tax policy affects incentives for work, saving, and investment. During recessions, governments often increase spending or cut taxes to stimulate demand, a policy approach known as Keynesian economics. The resulting budget deficits must eventually be addressed through future fiscal adjustment.

International trade allows countries to specialize in producing goods and services where they have comparative advantage and import what others produce more efficiently. Trade theory suggests that all countries can gain from international exchange even if one country is more efficient at producing everything. In practice, trade creates winners and losers within countries, generating political tensions around trade policy.

Labor economics studies the determinants of wages, employment, and working conditions. Supply and demand in labor markets determine wages, but institutional factors including minimum wage laws, unions, and discrimination also play important roles. Technological change has historically created new jobs while eliminating others, though the pace and nature of automation-driven disruption in the current period is a subject of active debate among economists.

Behavioral economics incorporates insights from psychology to explain why people sometimes make decisions that deviate from the predictions of standard rational choice theory. People systematically overestimate small probabilities, are averse to losses relative to equivalent gains, and are strongly influenced by how choices are framed and presented. These findings have important implications for the design of financial products, public policies, and organizational incentive systems.

Development economics examines the economic conditions of low-income countries and the factors that drive economic development. Researchers study the roles of institutions, geography, education, health, and technology in determining why some countries are rich and others poor. Foreign aid, microfinance, conditional cash transfers, and other interventions have been evaluated through randomized controlled trials to identify effective approaches to reducing poverty.

The economics of information investigates how asymmetric information between buyers and sellers affects market outcomes. George Akerlof's analysis of the used car market showed how information asymmetry can cause markets to collapse. Principal-agent problems arise when one party acts on behalf of another but has different incentives and information. Mechanism design theory asks how institutions and contracts should be designed to align incentives and achieve desirable outcomes.
