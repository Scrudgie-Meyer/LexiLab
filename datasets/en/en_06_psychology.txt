Psychology is the scientific study of the mind and behavior. Researchers investigate mental processes including perception, cognition, emotion, personality, and social behavior. Clinical psychologists diagnose and treat mental disorders, while experimental psychologists conduct laboratory research on fundamental psychological processes. The field draws on biology, neuroscience, philosophy, and social science to understand what drives human thought and action.

Cognitive psychology examines mental processes such as attention, memory, language, problem solving, and decision making. Researchers use laboratory experiments, brain imaging techniques, and computational models to study how the mind processes information. Memory research has revealed that human memory is reconstructive rather than reproductive, meaning that remembering involves actively rebuilding past experiences rather than simply retrieving stored recordings.

Social psychology investigates how people's thoughts, feelings, and behaviors are influenced by others. Classic experiments by Stanley Milgram on obedience and Solomon Asch on conformity revealed the powerful influence of social pressure on individual behavior. Social psychologists study phenomena including attitude formation, persuasion, group dynamics, stereotyping, and prejudice, with applications to public health, law, and organizational management.

Developmental psychology studies how humans change across the lifespan. Researchers investigate physical, cognitive, emotional, and social development from infancy through old age. Jean Piaget's theory of cognitive development described how children progress through qualitatively different stages of thinking. Attachment theory, developed by John Bowlby, explained the fundamental human need for close emotional bonds and how early attachment experiences shape later relationships.

Neuroscience seeks to understand the nervous system and brain at multiple levels of analysis. Modern neuroimaging techniques including functional magnetic resonance imaging allow researchers to observe brain activity while participants perform cognitive tasks. These methods have revealed the neural basis of perception, memory, emotion, and decision making. Research on neuroplasticity has shown that the brain retains the capacity for significant structural change throughout life.
