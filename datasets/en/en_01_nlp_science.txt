Natural language processing is a subfield of linguistics, computer science, and artificial intelligence concerned with the interactions between computers and human language. The ultimate objective of natural language processing is to enable computers to understand, interpret, and generate human language in a way that is both meaningful and useful. Over the past two decades, the field has experienced remarkable progress driven by advances in machine learning and the availability of large text corpora.

Statistical approaches to natural language processing emerged as a dominant paradigm in the nineteen eighties and nineties. These methods rely on probabilistic models trained on large text corpora to learn patterns of language use. The success of statistical approaches demonstrated that many complex linguistic phenomena could be captured through careful analysis of word co-occurrence statistics and distributional patterns in text.

Word frequency analysis is one of the most fundamental techniques in computational linguistics. Zipf's law states that the frequency of a word is inversely proportional to its rank in the frequency table. This means that the most frequent word occurs approximately twice as often as the second most frequent word, three times as often as the third, and so on. Remarkably, this power law distribution holds across virtually all natural languages and text genres.

Collocation detection is the task of identifying fixed or semi-fixed multi-word expressions that occur more frequently together than would be expected by chance. Statistical association measures such as pointwise mutual information, chi-square, log-likelihood ratio, and t-score are commonly used to score bigram collocations. Each measure has different strengths and is sensitive to different aspects of word co-occurrence patterns.

Topic modeling provides a way to discover hidden thematic structure in large collections of documents. Latent Dirichlet Allocation represents each document as a mixture of topics and each topic as a distribution over words. These probabilistic models are widely applied to analyze scientific publications, news archives, and social media data to understand what people are writing and talking about.

Word embeddings represent words as dense vectors in a continuous vector space where semantically similar words are mapped to nearby points. Word2Vec and GloVe were early influential models for learning word embeddings from large text corpora. These distributed representations capture rich semantic relationships and syntactic patterns in language and have become fundamental building blocks for many natural language processing systems.

The transformer architecture introduced in the paper Attention Is All You Need revolutionized natural language processing. Models based on transformers, such as BERT and GPT, achieve human-level performance on many language understanding tasks. The self-attention mechanism allows the model to weigh the importance of different words in the context of the entire input sequence when processing each word.

Text classification is one of the most well-studied tasks in natural language processing. The goal is to assign predefined categories or labels to text documents based on their content. Sentiment analysis, spam detection, topic categorization, and language identification are common text classification applications. Modern deep learning methods significantly outperform traditional machine learning approaches on most classification benchmarks.

Information extraction aims to automatically extract structured information from unstructured text. Named entity recognition identifies mentions of persons, organizations, locations, and other entities in text. Relation extraction identifies semantic relationships between named entities. These capabilities enable the construction of knowledge graphs from large text collections.

Machine translation is the task of automatically translating text from one natural language to another. Neural machine translation systems based on encoder-decoder architectures with attention mechanisms have dramatically improved translation quality. Large pretrained multilingual models can translate between hundreds of language pairs, including low-resource languages with limited training data.
