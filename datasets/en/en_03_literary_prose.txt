The old man sat at the edge of the sea and watched the waves come and go as they had always done, as they would always do long after he was gone. He had lived beside this water for seventy years, and still it surprised him sometimes with its patience. The sea did not hurry. The sea did not worry. It simply continued, endlessly, doing what seas have always done.

His hands were rough from decades of work, the skin darkened by sun and wind until it seemed part of the landscape itself. He remembered when those same hands were smooth and quick, when they could mend nets faster than any man in the village. That was before the accident, before the years changed everything that could be changed.

The fishing boats were coming in now, small dark shapes against the evening light. He could name each boat from a distance, could tell by the way they sat in the water whether the catch had been good or poor. Today they rode high, which was not a good sign. The fish had been scarce lately. The young men complained about it constantly, but he had seen scarcity before and had seen abundance return.

His granddaughter came down the path carrying a basket and wearing the yellow dress she always wore when she wanted to seem cheerful. She was a kind girl, though she worried too much. She had her grandmother's eyes and her father's stubbornness, which was a difficult combination but not an unhappy one.

She sat beside him on the rock and opened the basket to reveal bread and cheese and two bottles of beer. They ate in companionable silence, watching the boats draw closer to shore. The evening was mild, the kind of evening that made it easy to believe that everything would be fine.

He thought about his wife, as he did every evening at this hour. She had loved this time of day particularly, the moment when the light went golden and the world seemed briefly suspended between afternoon and night. She would have enjoyed this evening. She would have had something funny to say about the fish being scarce, something that would have made him laugh despite himself.

The first boat reached the dock, and they could hear the shouts of the men unloading the catch. It was indeed thin, the catch. He watched his granddaughter's face and saw her make the calculations that young people with families always had to make, counting what there was against what was needed.

He remembered his own father teaching him that the sea was not an enemy, not even when it withheld its gifts. The sea simply was what it was, and a fisherman's job was to understand it, not to fight it. Understanding came slowly, over years, through failures as much as through successes.

The stars were beginning to appear. He would not sleep well tonight, he knew. The old rarely did, carrying too many memories to rest easily. But there was a kind of freedom in the wakefulness too, those quiet hours when the world slept and he could think whatever he wanted to think without anyone watching.

His granddaughter took his hand as they walked back to the village. She said nothing, which was the right thing to say. She was learning, he thought. Learning was the most important thing, and it never really stopped.
